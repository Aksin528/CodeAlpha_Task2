What is Phishing and How is it Done?

Phishing attack diagram: How scammers cast their nets?

Phishing is a type of cybercrime that uses fake emails or messages to steal your sensitive information (identity, passwords, credit card numbers, etc.). Just like a fisherman casting a line, cybercriminals 'bait' you to fall into their trap.

Tactics Used by Attackers:

  • Creating fake websites and applications.
  • Using domain spoofing to make messages look legitimate.
  • Using Generative AI to create realistic and flawless messages.
  • Manipulating links to make URLs appear correct.
  • Sending emails from trusted infrastructures that can bypass spam filters.
  • Using multiple channels such as SMS text messages, phone calls, and social media.

Different Types of Phishing

Phishing attacks have different names based on their targets and methods. Here are the most common ones:

1. Website Spoofing

An exact replica of a real website (e.g., a bank's site) is created. Users are redirected to this fake site, and all the information they enter is stolen by scammers.

2. Account Deactivation

Panic is created by threatening that the user's account will be closed or disabled. In this panic, the user is asked to enter their login information.

3. Advance-Fee Scam

Similar to the "Nigerian Prince" scam, a large gain is promised in exchange for a small advance payment. Once the payment is made, the promised money never arrives.

4. Spear Phishing

A personalized and highly effective type of attack that targets a specific individual or company.

5. Clone Phishing

Done by copying a previously sent legitimate email and making the link or file inside malicious.

6. Whaling

Attacks that target high-profile individuals such as CEOs and senior executives, often involving demands for large money transfers.

Fake sites often look exactly like the real ones. Pay attention to the URL!

No matter how advanced the methods used by cybercriminals are, you can protect yourself with a few simple tips:

  • STOP and VERIFY

    Be cautious of messages that create a sense of urgency. Before clicking on a link, verify the reliability of the source through official channels.

  • Check the URL

    When you visit a website, make sure the address bar starts with 'HTTPS' and shows a lock icon. Also, check if the URL looks correct.

  • Report Suspicious Activity

    Immediately report any suspicious emails or messages you receive to your IT department or the relevant unit. This helps protect others as well.

Real-World Phishing Examples üé£

Now that you know the tactics, here are a few examples of common phishing scams:

Example 1: Bank Account Alert

Sender: `support@bankofamerica.com.secure-update.net`

"Dear Customer, We have detected suspicious activity on your account. To prevent your account from being locked, please click the link below to verify your information immediately."

Why is it phishing? The message creates a sense of urgency and uses a non-standard domain (`.net`). You should never click on the link; instead, go directly to your bank's official website.

Example 2: Shipping Notification

Sender: `fedex@tracking-info.co`

"Your package is being held due to an unpaid shipping fee. Please complete the payment for your shipment to be delivered. Your tracking number is: #472910."

Why is it phishing? The sender's address is not the official courier company's domain (`.co`). Even if you are expecting a package, you should go to the official courier company's website and check your tracking number there.

Example 3: Social Media Password Reset

Sender: `no-reply@instagram.com.reset-password.net`

"An unrecognized device has been detected logging into your account. To secure your account, please reset your password. Please renew your password via the link below."

Why is it phishing? This email contains urgency and threat elements. Also, it uses a subdomain outside the official `instagram.com` domain.
